The Starting Question

What if the problem was never the content?

Netflix has one of the most sophisticated recommendation engines in the world. It knows your watch history, your genre preferences, the shows you abandoned at episode three. And yet 35% of sessions end without anything being watched. Users open the app, browse for fifteen to twenty minutes, and leave.

The easy assumption is that this is a content problem. Not enough good titles. Wrong genres. Poor localisation. That assumption shaped how most product thinking around streaming had been framed. This project started with a different hypothesis: what if the content was fine, and the problem was something else entirely?

Research

Three hypotheses. One winner.

Primary research across 32 participants: 10 in-depth interviews and a survey split across 22 general viewers and a follow-up vibe check cohort. The interviews focused on journey mapping from the moment a user opened Netflix to the moment they pressed play or gave up.

Three hypotheses were tested: content quality as the core problem, discoverability and surface area, and decision friction before playback. The research was designed to let any of the three win.

Hypothesis 01
Content Quality
Not enough good titles, wrong genres, poor localisation. Tested but not validated as P0.
Hypothesis 02
Discoverability
Surface area and search quality failing users. Partially validated but not primary.
Hypothesis 03 - Winner
Decision Friction
Cognitive load of choosing was exhausting users before they reached content they would enjoy.
Key Finding

The algorithm was not the bottleneck.

Decision friction won clearly. Users were not leaving because they could not find content they would enjoy. They were leaving because the process of choosing was exhausting. The cognitive load of evaluating title after title, reading synopses, starting trailers, second-guessing choices, accumulated until abandonment felt easier than committing.

"Algorithm sophistication had outpaced the user's ability to process what the algorithm was surfacing."

The pattern was consistent across interview subjects: a user would open Netflix with genuine intent to watch something, spend fifteen to twenty minutes browsing, feel increasingly overwhelmed, and either settle for something already seen or close the app. The content was there. The decision was the problem.

Prioritisation

RICE scoring. Four solutions. One dropped.

Four solutions were evaluated across Reach, Impact, Confidence, and Effort. Review Integration scored 4. Explore Toggle and Swipeable Reels both scored 9. AI Chat scored 2 and was deprioritised: effort was high, confidence in user adoption was low, and it addressed a different problem than the validated P0.

Adding a conversational interface to a platform people open wanting to relax was solving for a persona that did not appear strongly in the research.

Solutions

Three solutions targeting the moment before play.

Review Integration pulls social proof signals into the browsing experience. A user sees aggregated ratings and short reviews from people with similar watch history before committing to a title. It reduces the uncertainty of choosing something unknown without requiring the user to leave the platform.

Explore Toggle gives users two modes: Quick Pick for users who want the algorithm to make a fast recommendation based on their current session, and Browse All for users who want to scan. The mode selection itself is a signal the algorithm can learn from over time.

Swipeable Reels introduces a micro-preview format. Users swipe through short clips rather than reading synopses. Left swipe is an explicit negative signal, which is data Netflix currently does not collect systematically. The format reduces cognitive load by replacing text-based decision-making with immediate, visceral response.

Metrics

Before, after, and what we guard against.

North Star
Time to Watch-Start - session open to play button press
Before
20 min average browse time / 20% watch-start rate / 35% abandonment
Target
Under 5 min browse time / 40%+ watch-start rate / under 20% abandonment
Guardrails
Long-form completion rate must not drop. Rewatch rate must not rise disproportionately.
Roadmap

12 months. 5 phases. One bet.

1
Months 1-2: P0 Foundation
Instrument session abandonment tracking, establish baselines, run qualitative validation with a small cohort.
2
Months 3-5: Review Integration
Build and launch review panels for the top 20% of catalog by viewing volume. Measure before expanding.
3
Months 4-6: Explore Toggle
Launch Quick Pick and Browse All modes. Instrument mode selection as a behavioural signal.
4
Months 6-9: Swipeable Reels
Launch micro-preview format for a content subset. Collect left-swipe data and validate as negative signal.
5
Months 9-12: Integration and Scale
Combine signals from all three solutions into a unified contextual layer. Expand to full catalog.

"Reducing decision friction before playback will unlock more user and business value than adding more content or deepening personalisation."
