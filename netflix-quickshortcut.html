The Gap

Part 1 solved discovery. Not context.

Part 1 ended with a roadmap. Three solutions, five phases, a north star metric. On paper it was complete. But there was a problem none of those solutions fully resolved. The Explore Toggle helps users who know their mood. Swipeable Reels helps users who need to browse faster. Review Integration helps users who need social proof. All three reduce friction after the algorithm has already made its recommendations. None of them change what the algorithm is working from.

Netflix's recommendation engine is sophisticated at learning long-term taste. What it cannot do is read the session a user is in right now. It does not know if they have 40 minutes or three hours. It does not know if they are exhausted or alert. The algorithm knows history. It has no mechanism for the present moment.

The Problem

Taste problem vs. context problem.

The recommendation system operates on revealed preference: what you have watched and rated before. It asks: given everything this person has chosen in the past, what should they choose next? That is a reasonable question. But it is the wrong question when what a user needs is: given how I feel right now and how much time I have, what should I watch tonight?

"Netflix solves taste well. Nobody solves context. That gap is the specific failure mode Quick Shortcut was designed to close."

Users who know roughly what kind of experience they want - something light, under 90 minutes, low concentration - cannot efficiently surface it because the interface is organised around historical taste rather than present-moment state.

The Solution

Removal, not addition.

Quick Shortcut is a context-aware discovery layer that sits between the user's stated intent and Netflix's catalog. It is not a search bar. It is not a chatbot. It is not a replacement for existing recommendation rows. It is a lightweight intent-capture and matching engine that asks one question: what kind of session is this?

The design principle is removal, not addition. The goal is not to give users more options. It is to give them fewer, better ones, calibrated to right now rather than to their entire watch history.

Design Decision

Why two structured questions beat a text box.

The first instinct when designing an intent-capture system is to give users a text box. That feels more powerful and more flexible. The prototype made a different call: two structured questions instead of an open prompt.

1
How much time do you have?
Quick (15-30 min) / Episode (30-60 min) / Movie (90 min+) / Not Sure Yet
2
What vibe are you going for?
Light and Fun / Thrilling and Intense / Romantic / Action-Packed / Thought-Provoking / Surprise Me

Three reasons this was the right call. First, most users arriving at a streaming platform do not have a precisely articulated intent - they have a rough feeling. Structured options meet them where they are. Second, free text creates enormous variance in how people express intent, making AI matching less reliable. Third, the two-question flow takes under 20 seconds versus the friction of a thoughtful text prompt.

What Was Built

A live system, not a mockup.

The prototype was built and deployed on Google Cloud Run. Users move through the two-step intent capture, see an AI scanning transition, and arrive at a results page surfacing a hero recommendation with a match percentage, a vibe label, and a row of additional AI picks. The results page is intentionally Netflix-native in look and feel.

"AI picked these based on your moment."

The framing on every result reinforces the core promise: this is not your history speaking, it is your right now. Decision time in prototype testing moved from a baseline of approximately 20 minutes to under three minutes.

Metrics

What success looks like and what it cannot cost.

North Star
Decision Time - session open to play. Target: 20 min to under 3 min.
Intent Capture
% of sessions where user engages with Quick Shortcut prompt rather than dismissing it
Prediction Quality
Accuracy across vibe and time combinations, validated via post-session ratings
Fallback Rate
% engaging with Quick Shortcut then returning to manual browse - clearest signal that filtering is failing
Guardrail
Long-form completion rate must not drop - Quick Shortcut cannot optimise for low-commitment picks
The Bigger Picture

Quick Shortcut is Phase 4 of the Part 1 roadmap.

Quick Shortcut is not a parallel product. It is the intelligent evolution of the discovery system designed in Part 1. The explicit feedback signals from Swipeable Reels feed the vibe matching layer. The intent signals from the Explore Toggle reveal which sessions are high-certainty versus exploratory.

In the Part 1 roadmap, Phases 1 through 3 build the feedback infrastructure. Quick Shortcut is what that infrastructure makes possible: a system that has enough signal to stop asking users to navigate and start anticipating what they need.
