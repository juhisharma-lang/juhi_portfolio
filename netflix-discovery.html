THE STARTING QUESTION

What if the problem was never the content?

Netflix has one of the most sophisticated recommendation engines in the world. It knows your watch history, your genre preferences, the shows you abandoned at episode three. And yet 35% of sessions end without anything being watched. Users open the app, browse for fifteen to twenty minutes, and leave.

The easy assumption is that this is a content problem. Not enough good titles. Wrong genres. Poor localisation. That assumption shaped how most product thinking around streaming had been framed. This project started with a different hypothesis: what if the content was fine, and the problem was something else entirely?

RESEARCH

Three hypotheses. One winner.

Primary research across 32 participants: 10 in-depth interviews and a survey split across 22 general viewers and a follow-up vibe check cohort. The interviews focused on journey mapping from the moment a user opened Netflix to the moment they pressed play or gave up.

Three hypotheses were tested: content quality as the core problem, discoverability and surface area, and decision friction before playback. The research was designed to let any of the three win.

HYPOTHESIS 01

Content Quality

Not enough good titles, wrong genres, poor localisation. Tested but not validated as P0.

HYPOTHESIS 02

Discoverability

Surface area and search quality failing users. Partially validated but not primary.

HYPOTHESIS 03 — WINNER

Decision Friction

Cognitive load of choosing was exhausting users before they reached content they would enjoy.

KEY FINDING

The algorithm was not the bottleneck.

Decision friction won clearly. Users were not leaving because they could not find content they would enjoy. They were leaving because the process of choosing was exhausting. The cognitive load of evaluating title after title, reading synopses, starting trailers, second-guessing choices, accumulated until abandonment felt easier than committing.

"Algorithm sophistication had outpaced the user's ability to process what the algorithm was surfacing."

The pattern was consistent across interview subjects: a user would open Netflix with genuine intent to watch something, spend fifteen to twenty minutes browsing, feel increasingly overwhelmed, and either settle for something already seen or close the app. The content was there. The decision was the problem.

THE GAP

Taste problem vs. context problem.

The recommendation system operates on revealed preference: what you have watched and rated before. It asks: given everything this person has chosen in the past, what should they choose next? That is a reasonable question. But it is the wrong question when what a user needs is: given how I feel right now and how much time I have, what should I watch tonight?

"Netflix solves taste well. Nobody solves context. That gap is the specific failure mode Quick Shortcut was designed to close."

Users who know roughly what kind of experience they want — something light, under 90 minutes, low concentration — cannot efficiently surface it because the interface is organised around historical taste rather than present-moment state.

PRIORITISATION

RICE scoring. Four solutions. One dropped.

Four solutions were evaluated across Reach, Impact, Confidence, and Effort. Review Integration scored 4. Explore Toggle and Swipeable Reels both scored 9. AI Chat scored 2 and was deprioritised: effort was high, confidence in user adoption was low, and it addressed a different problem than the validated P0.

Adding a conversational interface to a platform people open wanting to relax was solving for a persona that did not appear strongly in the research.

SOLUTIONS

Three solutions targeting the moment before play.

Three solutions, each reducing a different kind of friction at a different point in the same journey.

Internal Social Proof

Pulls engagement signals into the browsing experience — trending this week, completion rates, save counts — sourced entirely from Netflix-owned data. No external APIs. A user sees aggregated signals before committing to a title, reducing the uncertainty of choosing something unknown.

Swipeable Reels

Introduces a micro-preview format. Users swipe through content cards rather than reading synopses. Left swipe is an explicit negative signal, which is data Netflix currently does not collect systematically. The format reduces cognitive load by replacing text-based decision-making with immediate, visceral response.

AI Quick Shortcut

A context-aware discovery layer that sits between the user's stated intent and Netflix's catalog. It is not a search bar. It is not a chatbot. It is not a replacement for existing recommendation rows. It is a lightweight intent-capture and matching engine that asks one question: what kind of session is this?

The design principle is removal, not addition. The goal is not to give users more options. It is to give them fewer, better ones, calibrated to right now rather than to their entire watch history.

DESIGN DECISION

Why two structured questions beat a text box.

The first instinct when designing an intent-capture system is to give users a text box. That feels more powerful and more flexible. The prototype made a different call: three structured questions instead of an open prompt.

QUESTION 1

How much time do you have?

Quick (under 30 min) / One episode (30–60 min) / Settling in (90 min+) / Not sure

QUESTION 2

How are you feeling?

Happy / Calm / Tense / Sad / Excited / Tired / Curious / Romantic

QUESTION 3

What kind of story?

A gripping story / Something funny / True stories / Action & spectacle / Romance & relationships / Mind-bending / Something light / Surprise me

Three reasons this was the right call. First, most users arriving at a streaming platform do not have a precisely articulated intent — they have a rough feeling. Structured options meet them where they are. Second, free text creates enormous variance in how people express intent, making AI matching less reliable. Third, the three-question flow takes under 30 seconds versus the friction of a thoughtful text prompt.

WHAT WAS BUILT

A live system, not a mockup.

The prototype was built in React and TypeScript, deployed on Vercel. Users move through three-step intent capture — time, emotion, story type — see an AI scanning transition, and arrive at a results page surfacing a hero recommendation with context chips showing exactly what they chose, a row of best matches, and a stretch row for deliberate discovery outside the stated mood.

The Reels feature sits alongside the shortcut flow as a separate entry point — swipe left to pass, swipe right to save, keyboard navigation supported for demos. Social proof badges appear on the browse homepage only, not on results rows, where the AI pick is the signal.

"AI picked these based on your moment."

The framing on every result reinforces the core promise: this is not your history speaking, it is your right now.

METRICS

What success looks like and what it cannot cost.

NORTH STAR

Decision Time

Session open to play. Target: 20 min to under 3 min.

INTENT CAPTURE

% of sessions where user engages with Quick Shortcut prompt rather than dismissing it

PREDICTION QUALITY

Accuracy across mood and story type combinations, validated via post-session ratings

FALLBACK RATE

% engaging with Quick Shortcut then returning to manual browse — clearest signal that filtering is failing

GUARDRAIL

Long-form completion rate must not drop. Quick Shortcut cannot optimise for low-commitment picks.

THE BIGGER PICTURE

Quick Shortcut completes what the roadmap started.

Quick Shortcut is not a parallel product. It is the intelligent evolution of the discovery system. The explicit feedback signals from Swipeable Reels feed the matching layer. The intent signals from the shortcut flow reveal which sessions are high-certainty versus exploratory.

The three features work as a system: Reels collects what users reject, Social Proof reduces uncertainty at the point of choice, and Quick Shortcut eliminates the browse phase for users who already know roughly what they want. Each one reduces a different kind of friction at a different point in the same journey.

"Reducing decision friction before playback will unlock more user and business value than adding more content or deepening personalisation."
